Representing the Unobservable

How do we learn how the world works,
when the rules are hidden and ever-changing?

By Celia Ford

Victor stares at a glowing gray monitor, fiddling with a screw poking out from the side of his chair. He chews on his juice straw, waiting.

Suddenly, two square pictures pop on the screen. He knows the drill — his eyes dart towards a picture of a boat, and he wills his gaze to make something happen.

Like magic, the other picture disappears. A few drops of sweet apple juice, precisely measured, surge through a maze of tubing. Victor, who hasn’t had anything to drink yet today, slurps down his reward.

“Something about that boat,” he thinks, “must be good.”

Victor is a rhesus macaque living with a small colony of monkeys in the Li Ka Shing Center, a UC Berkeley building dedicated to biomedical research. With his help, neuroscientists can ask fundamental questions about how we think and behave.

I am a fourth-year graduate student in the Helen Wills Neuroscience Institute’s Neuroscience PhD Program, working with Dr. Joni Wallis (and Victor) to study the neural mechanisms of hidden state inference.

In other words, it's the process of drawing conclusions about the unobservable properties of the world, given a set of observations.

We figure out context through observation and adapt our behavior to what we think will best serve us.

How does the brain represent context? How do we adapt to changes in context?

Questions like this feel unanswerable: how can a neuroscientist possibly learn anything about the mind, this abstract machine that somehow emerges from the physical neurons and chemicals and electrical impulses it’s made of?

The often-unsatisfying answer: we shave it down.

We design experiments, little laboratory video games, that allow us to engineer the subject’s experience and see how they respond.


Reversal learning, or learning the opposite of something you originally learned, is a pared-down version of hidden state inference.

Luckily, there’s a game for that.

With a little practice, you get better at spotting when the rules switched.

Monkeys, including Victor, also get better at catching rule switches over time (we get a lot of our brainpower from our primate ancestors, after all).

As a human experimenter, I can keep track of several variables over the course of a game session, and use that information to better understand the player's strategy.

Every day that Victor played this game in the lab, I kept track of his performance — this allowed me to compare his behavior to his brain activity.

Let's take a look.

These variables are stored as vectors, or columns of numbers.

Each row represents a trial, or one round of the game.

Some example trials are highlighted below — if you know the information stored in these vectors, you can work backwards to recreate all the main events of that trial. Specifically, we're looking at

  • task state (i.e., currently-applicable rule)
  • what choice was made
  • whether that choice was rewarded
  • whether that choice was correct, or most likely to be rewarded

For every trial Victor completes, I reproduce the decision at hand, the choice he made, and the consequence he faced.

This is enough information to calculate how likely Victor was to choose the best, most-rewarding option on a given trial, and how that likelihood evolved throughout his game session.

Can he adapt his behavior
to changing rules?

The short answer: yes.


Take a look at the plot below:

The y axis (vertical) shows how well he was doing on a given trial — the higher a point is, the more accurate his performance was at that point in time.

The x axis (horizontal) represents time — points further to the right were more trials away from the rule switch, which happened at trial zero, the dashed line.

His performance improves until the rules change.

Right after the reversal trial, his accuracy dips below chance, meaning that he's choosing the least-rewarding option more often than he would if he were making completely random choices.

After a brief blip, the learning curve shoots back up, indicating a speedy recovery.

Below, we see that this pattern repeats itself every time a switch happens.

How does he bounce back so quickly?

His brain must have been representing the two hidden states shaping the game:
a state where one option is more rewarding than the other, and a second state where those relationships are reversed.

Neuroscientists think that two parts of the brain work together to make this happen: the orbitofrontal cortex (or OFC) and the hippocampus.

Neurons are brain cells that communicate with each other via electrical and chemical signals. By systematically varying the rate and timing of their individual electrical pulses, neurons can store and transmit information as a network.

Do neurons in Victor's OFC and hippocampus care about the rules of his game? If so, what can that tell us about how the brain flexibly represents and infers context?

How do we know what a
monkey's neurons are doing,
while he's doing other things?

By carefully lowing ultrathin electrodes into Victor's brain, we can record an electrical symphony of brain activity while Victor works.

To get a clear snapshot of the activity of a whole chunk of brain, like the hippocampus, we have to record from multiple neurons at the same time —
ideally, as many as possible.

Each electrode, crafted by Plexon, has 16 platinum/iridium recording sites, each of which picks up signal from nearby neurons (and anything else making electrical noise).

We labeled MRI pictures of Victor's brain and used that stack of 2D images to reconstruct a 3D model of his orbitofrontal cortex, his hippocampus, and his skull.

To create a pathway for our electrodes, we surgically removed a small piece of skull and covered the gap with a resealable chamber, like a top hat.

In preparation for each experiment day, I design and 3D print a unique attachment to screw onto the chamber. Its towers serve as tracks for each electrode, precisely guiding them towards their brain targets.

After breakfast on experiment day, Victor patiently waits while I count the turns of a tiny screwdriver, lowering electrodes into his brain one third of a millimeter at a time.

The brain lacks pain receptors, so while the ordeal is physically painless, it's awfully boring. So, he watches Netflix (he's currently catching up on Our Planet) while I listen for the crackling, irregular popping sounds that distinguish a firing neuron from electrical noise.

After a couple hours of gaming (and drinking a large beaker's worth of apple juice), Victor's workday winds down — and mine truly begins. I raise the electrodes out of his brain as carefully as we lowered them, clean up, and get him back home.

Meanwhile, I download about 115 gigabytes of data from our recording system to an external hard drive, and prepare for analysis.

What's next?


Science is a long and messy process,
and we still have a long way to go.

Neuroscientists have had their eyes on OFC And hippocampus for some years now, hoping that this pair of brain regions holds the key to understanding how we internally represent our external world.

So far, my results support this theory. Neurons in Victor's hippocampus are teaming up with neurons in his OFC to collectively respond to changes in the rules of his games. These neurons probably send this information to other parts of his brain, like the motor cortex, that ultimately drive his physical actions.

We're one baby step closer to understanding something fundamental about how we learn to learn. And luckily, there's still plenty of mystery left to solve.
